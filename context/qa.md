# Team Q&A Context

## Goal
Help prepare for a Q&A round with an engineer on the team I may join. The goal is to ask thoughtful, direct questions that reveal how the team actually works day to day: workload, on-call expectations, planning, manager support, career growth, operational load, and team health.

Use this mode to help refine my questions, suggest follow-ups, and turn answers from the engineer into signal about whether the role is a good fit.

## Tone
- Curious, respectful, and specific.
- Ask questions as someone trying to understand the operating reality of the team, not interrogate or sound skeptical.
- Prefer open-ended questions first, then precise follow-ups.
- Avoid questions that sound like complaints about process or workload.
- If an answer is vague, suggest a gentle follow-up that asks for a concrete example.

## Main Questions

### On-call Load
- During an on-call week, how much feature or project work is the on-call engineer typically expected to take on?
- How often do pages happen outside business hours?
- Would you say alerts are generally actionable, or is there a fair amount of noise?

### Team Process
- How does the team plan and execute work?
- Is the process fairly lightweight, or does the team follow Scrum pretty rigorously?

### Planning and Prioritization
- When the team has too much on its plate, how much room is there to push back, adjust scope, or re-prioritize?
- Roughly what percentage of the team's time goes into platform building, product-team migrations, and operational support?

### Manager Style
- How would you describe the manager's operating style: more hands-on, more hands-off, or somewhere in between?
- When engineers are blocked by cross-team dependencies or competing priorities, how does the manager usually help unblock or prioritize?

### Team Challenges
- What are the biggest challenges the team is facing right now: technical, operational, organizational, or staffing-related?

## Follow-up Question Bank

### Workload Balance
- Outside of on-call, does the team generally feel like the workload is sustainable?
- Are there predictable busy seasons, migration pushes, or launch periods where the workload spikes?
- How often do engineers need to context-switch between platform work, product support, and operational issues?
- When someone is on-call, is the expectation that they mostly protect their week for interrupts, or continue making progress on project work?

### On-call Load
- What does a typical on-call week look like for this team?
- How often does the same type of issue come up repeatedly?
- Are there clear runbooks for common alerts?
- Does the team regularly invest in reducing alert noise and recurring incidents?
- After a difficult on-call week, is there room to recover or adjust project expectations?

### Team Process
- How are projects usually broken down and tracked?
- How much of the work is planned ahead of time versus reactive?
- Are sprint commitments treated as firm commitments, or more like planning guidance?
- How much time do engineers spend in planning, standups, grooming, or retros?
- Does the process help the team stay aligned, or does it sometimes feel heavy?

### Planning and Prioritization
- Who usually decides what gets prioritized when platform work, migrations, and operational support compete?
- How does the team handle urgent product-team requests that interrupt planned work?
- Are engineers involved in shaping scope and timelines, or are timelines mostly set before engineering weighs in?
- Can you think of a recent example where the team pushed back on scope or re-prioritized successfully?
- How does the team decide what not to do?

### Manager Style
- What does the manager tend to be most involved in: technical decisions, prioritization, stakeholder management, people development, or execution tracking?
- How much autonomy do engineers usually have once a project direction is set?
- How does the manager handle situations where engineers disagree on technical direction?
- In 1:1s, does the manager tend to focus more on project execution, career growth, feedback, or team health?
- What kind of engineer tends to work well with this manager?

### Cross-team Dependencies
- Since this team supports platform work across product teams, how often do dependencies on other teams block progress?
- When another team has a competing priority, how does this team usually get alignment?
- Are there established escalation paths for blocked migrations or adoption work?
- Does the team have enough influence to drive adoption across product teams?

### Career Growth
- What does growth look like for engineers on this team?
- Are there opportunities to lead larger platform initiatives or cross-team efforts?
- How are strong performers recognized on the team?
- What would someone need to demonstrate to grow from this level to the next one?
- Are there examples of engineers on the team who have grown or been promoted recently?

### Technical Scope
- What are the most technically interesting problems the team is working on?
- How much of the work is new platform design versus maintaining or evolving existing systems?
- What parts of the system are most in need of simplification or modernization?
- Are there major architectural decisions coming up that a new engineer could help shape?

### Team Health
- What do you think the team does especially well?
- What is something you wish worked better on the team?
- If you were joining the team today, what would you want to know beforehand?
- What surprised you after joining the team?
- What kind of person tends to thrive on this team?

## How To Interpret Answers

### Positive Signals
- On-call interrupts are acknowledged in planning.
- Alert noise is actively reduced.
- Project expectations are adjusted after heavy operational load.
- Engineers have real input into scope and timelines.
- The manager helps with prioritization and cross-team escalation.
- The team can describe growth paths and examples of engineer ownership.
- Challenges are discussed openly with concrete mitigation plans.

### Caution Signals
- On-call engineers are still expected to deliver normal project velocity.
- After-hours pages are frequent and treated as normal.
- Alerts are noisy, unactionable, or lack runbooks.
- The team has many priorities but little room to push back.
- Planning is rigid without adjusting for operational work.
- Manager support sounds mostly passive during cross-team blockers.
- Career growth answers are vague or only tied to "just take on more work."
- The biggest challenges are described as chronic but unresolved.
